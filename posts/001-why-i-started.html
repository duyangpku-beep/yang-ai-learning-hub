Why I Started This Hub

On the unlikely kinship between calligraphy, law, and artificial intelligence.

I did not come to artificial intelligence through computer science. I came through books, arguments, and ink.

My formation was in the liberal arts — the kind of education that asks you to hold contradictions, to read primary sources, to write until the thought becomes clear rather than merely plausible. Then came law, which sharpened that into something more precise: the ability to construct an argument, to anticipate the counter, to know that language is never neutral. And running underneath all of it, for as long as I can remember, is traditional Chinese calligraphy — 书法 — the practice of returning each morning to the same strokes, the same characters, until the hand stops performing and starts understanding.

What any of this has to do with AI

More than you might expect.

The quality that liberal arts trains above all else is the ability to read carefully and to ask good questions. It turns out that is also, almost exactly, what separates a useful prompt from a useless one. When I write a prompt I am doing something very close to what I did when writing a legal brief: stating the problem precisely, anticipating how it might be misread, and giving enough context to make the answer I want the answer that makes sense.

The discipline of calligraphy and the art of prompting share the same secret: there is no shortcut through repetition. You must do it badly, many times, before you do it well.

Calligraphy taught me something subtler — that the process of learning is not linear. You plateau. You regress. You copy masters not to imitate them but to inhabit their intentions long enough to find your own. That is, I think, exactly what it feels like to learn to use AI well. The tools change every few months. The models get better. But the underlying question — what are you actually trying to say? — stays constant.

Why share it here

I built this hub because I wanted a place that reflected how I actually learn: eclectically, slowly, with detours. Most AI content online is written by engineers for engineers, or by hype merchants for everyone else. I wanted something in between — a record of a non-technical person taking this seriously, making mistakes, and occasionally finding something genuinely useful.

If you share any of these building blocks — an interest in reading, in craft, in thinking carefully about technology — I hope something here is useful to you. 有缘相见。
