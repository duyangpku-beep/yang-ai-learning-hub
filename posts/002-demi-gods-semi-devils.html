The Parallel World of Demi-Gods and Semi-Devils

On Jin Yong, wuxia, and why great stories still make people forget their phones.

During the Spring Festival, while many were immersed in AI, I found myself diving deep into Jin Yong's Demi-Gods and Semi-Devils — plunging headfirst into the parallel world he created. I hid behind a cloud floating above that land, watching the jianghu (the martial arts world) unfold below: vivid characters, mystical martial arts, chivalrous righteousness, love and hatred. I felt infinite longing, yet also relief that I didn't have to expose myself to that perilous world where everyone knows kung fu.

My daughter, seeing me clutching a book all day, asked curiously what I was reading. I told her the story of Duan Yu falling off the cliff of Wuliang Mountain and accidentally discovering the secret realm of the Carefree Sect. She listened, mesmerized, her eyes shining as she said, "Mom, this is the best story I've ever heard!"

On one hand, I secretly rejoiced — Grandpa Jin Yong's charm still works across half a century. On the other, a tinge of regret surfaced in my heart: How could such a magnificent story seem forgotten by our era?

Foreigners have their parallel worlds

Harry Potter's Hogwarts, Star Wars' Galactic Empire, Dune's Arrakis, or Game of Thrones' King's Landing. For Chinese people, at least for our generation, the softest, most soaring fantasy embedded in our marrow is undoubtedly wuxia — the martial arts chivalry world.

There, we find martial arts masters with peerless qinggong (lightness skill), scaling walls and flying across rooftops. There's justice upheld in the jianghu, chivalrous bones with tender hearts, swift vengeance and settled scores. Foreigners have their magic; we have wuxia. This isn't merely entertainment — it's romanticism rooted deep in our culture.

Every time I open Jin Yong's book

It feels like leaping into water — a dong sound, and the real world disappears. The anxieties, trivialities, and worries of reality vanish. All that remains are tales of gratitude and grudges, love and hatred, and move after move of exquisite martial arts.

In the wuxia world, time flows differently. When you're truly immersed, you look up at the clock and two hours have passed. Isn't that time travel?

Recently, I heard someone say that short videos are killing reading. But I believe the immersive experience of entering a parallel world through reading is something short videos can never reach. A good book, a great story, can still make people forget their phones, forget time.

Thank you, Grandpa Jin Yong

Thank you, Grandpa Jin Yong, for leaving us these stories. They're not just wuxia — they're a unique romantic education for the Chinese soul.

My daughter's best story ever — I'll keep telling it to her. Book by book. Let those roof-leaping heroes, those clear-cut worlds of gratitude and grievance, become parallel worlds in her heart too.
